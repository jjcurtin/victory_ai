Pricing Framework

The "Anchor High, Negotiate to Your Floor" Model

Your Opening Ask: $500K — This is your "published rate" for the full pilot engagement including development, deployment, compliance, Kipu integration, training, and 90 days of managed support. It includes $150K in professional services value you won't get elsewhere.

Your Walk-Away Floor: $350K — This covers your actual build + compliance costs. You never go below this. If they push below $350K, you counter with scope reduction, not price reduction.

Your Expected Landing Zone: $400–$450K — This is where most successful health tech pilot negotiations settle. You give something, they feel like they won, and you're fully funded with margin.

What Successful Startups Do

The "Monkey's Paw" Structure

Charge for the pilot engagement upfront. Then offer: "If you convert to a full enterprise license within 6 months of pilot completion, we credit the $500K pilot fee against your first-year license." Now $500K isn't a cost — it's a deposit on a bigger deal. BayMark's CFO sees zero net cost if the pilot works.

Critical Principle

Never Offer Free

Per Rock Health's data and Dreamit's healthcare accelerator research: startups that don't charge for pilots get deprioritized by the hospital system. When BayMark pays, they assign resources, clear blockers, and have skin in the game. Free pilots die in committee. Paid pilots get executive sponsors.

03 — Internal Champion Strategy

Turn the VP of Ops into your evangelist.

He's not the final decision maker — he's more valuable than that. Here's the playbook to make him your inside champion who sells this up.

Champion Framework

The "Internal Evangelist" Model

Research from Washington University's CTSA and Dreamit Ventures confirms: in large healthcare systems, the champion who fights for your pilot internally is more important than the decision-maker who signs. Penn Medicine's innovation team, Intermountain Healthcare, and IBX all operate this way. Your VP of Ops needs three things from you:

1. A win he can claim. Frame VictoryAI as HIS innovation initiative. "You'd be the executive who brought AI-powered OUD outcomes to BayMark's California operations." Give him the narrative arc: he saw the opportunity, he ran the pilot, he delivered the data.

2. Air cover from risk. He's putting his reputation on the line. Give him a pilot structure where failure is impossible — bounded scope, clear success metrics, and an opt-out clause. He never has to defend a "failed" investment because the pilot is designed to generate publishable outcomes data regardless.

3. A deck he can forward. He'll need to sell this to Jason Kletter (President), the CFO, and likely the PE board. You need to hand him a 1-page executive summary so polished he can forward it with "Worth 15 minutes of your time" and look brilliant.

The exact words that close.

Adapted from frameworks used by Omada Health, Propeller Health, Hinge Health, and other DTx companies that landed first paid pilots with major systems.

Opening — The "Value Capture" Frame (60 seconds)
"[VP Name], I'll be direct — we've built something specifically for organizations like BayMark, and I want to show you why your California BAART clinics could generate an additional $1.2 million annually in EMR-billable data you're currently not capturing."

Pause. Let that number land.

"Right now, your OTP model captures methadone dosing, counseling sessions, and standard billing. "VictoryAI generates that data automatically. Every MAT dose confirmation, every craving log, every COWS score, every CBT session — all of it structured, timestamped, and exported to Kipu in a format your billing team can use the same day."
The Pivot — "This Isn't Software, It's Revenue Infrastructure" (90 seconds)
"But here's what makes this different from every digital health pitch you've heard — we're not asking BayMark to buy software. We're offering to build the data capture layer that funds itself."

"At $490 to $737 in identifiable billing data per patient per month, a 200-patient pilot across 3 to 5 BAART California sites would surface roughly $107K per month in previously uncaptured billable data. Over a 12-month enterprise rollout, that's $1.28M just from California."

"And we haven't even talked about the retention impact. Your patients drop out. Every MAT patient who leaves is lost recurring revenue. That alone changes the unit economics of every BAART clinic."

[Show the ROI calculator from your landing page — let him input BayMark's actual patient counts]
The Ask — "Joint Innovation Pilot" (60 seconds)
"What we're proposing is a 90-day Joint Innovation Pilot across 3 to 5 of your highest-volume California BAART sites. We handle everything: HIPAA compliance, 42 CFR Part 2, Kipu integration, clinician training, and ongoing support."

"The investment for the full pilot engagement — development, deployment, compliance, and managed support — is $500,000."

Pause. Don't fill the silence.

"And here's the part that makes this a no-brainer: if BayMark converts to a full enterprise deployment within 6 months of pilot completion, the entire pilot fee is credited against the enterprise license. So if this works — and we'll have the data to prove it — the pilot was free. If it doesn't hit the agreed metrics, you learned something valuable for $500K. Either way, BayMark wins."
The Champion Close (30 seconds)
"[VP Name], I think you see the opportunity here. Who else would need to see this data before we could move forward on a pilot scope? I'd love to put together a tailored brief for your leadership team — and if it's helpful, I'm happy to present alongside you."

This positions him as the driver. You're his resource, not his salesperson.

06 — Objection Handling

When they push back — you're ready.

Every objection is a buying signal. Here's how to handle each one.

"$500K is a lot for unproven technology."

Response: "I understand — and that's exactly why we structured it as a creditable pilot. If VictoryAI hits the agreed metrics and you go enterprise, this $500K becomes a credit, not a cost. You're essentially making a refundable deposit on $1.2M+ in annual billing data. The question isn't whether $500K is a lot — it's whether leaving $1.2M on the table every year is more expensive."

"Can we just do a free trial first?"

Response: "We've seen what happens with free trials in healthcare — they get deprioritized. Nobody assigns clinicians, nobody tracks data, and 6 months later we're still 'evaluating.' The pilot fee ensures both sides have skin in the game. BayMark assigns real clinical sites and real patients, we deliver production-grade technology with full Kipu integration. A free trial gets you a demo. A funded pilot gets you an outcomes study you can take to your PE board and to DHCS."

"We need to run this by our IT team / legal / PE board."

Response: "Absolutely — and I'd expect nothing less for an investment of this size. Can I help prepare materials for each of those stakeholders? I have a technical architecture brief for IT, a compliance summary for legal showing our HIPAA and 42 CFR Part 2 positioning, and a one-page ROI model for the board. Would it help if I joined those conversations with you?"

(This is where your champion strategy pays off — you're offering to arm him.)

"We're already looking at [competitor / building in-house]."

Response: "That's smart — you should evaluate every option. What I'd ask you to compare is time-to-value. An in-house build takes 18–24 months and $1M+ minimum before you see a single data point. A competitor without OUD-specific gamification, family engagement, and COWS/MAT integration is a general wellness app wearing a clinical hat. We've purpose-built every feature for the exact patient population in your BAART clinics. A 90-day pilot answers the question definitively — with data, not projections."

"Can you come down on price?"

Response: "I can adjust scope before I adjust price. If $500K is above your current budget authority, we could phase the pilot — start with 2 sites and 100 patients at $350K, with an option to expand to 5 sites at full scope. But I want to be transparent: the Kipu integration, HIPAA infrastructure, and compliance work are fixed costs regardless of patient count. The per-patient cost actually goes down significantly at 200+ patients."

(This is how you negotiate down to your $350K floor while anchoring to a reduced scope, not a reduced value.)

08 — Precedents & Patterns

How others did it first.

Real patterns from the digital therapeutics and health tech landscape that validate the paid-pilot-funds-development model.

The Pattern: Customer-Funded Development

Across the DTx landscape — Omada Health (diabetes), Hinge Health (MSK), Pear Therapeutics (SUD), SWORD Health (physical therapy), and Big Health (insomnia) — the most common early revenue model was paid pilots with large health systems that covered development costs. The playbook is consistent:

1. Land one named-brand pilot partner — the "lighthouse customer" whose name validates you to everyone else.
2. Structure the pilot as a "joint innovation" or "co-development" — not a vendor sale. The health system gets exclusivity, co-authorship on outcomes data, and the prestige of being first.
3. Price the pilot to cover development + margin — because the next 10 deals will reference this pilot's outcomes.
4. Use the outcomes data to raise your Series A at 3–5× better terms than you would have without it.

Pattern Match

Pear Therapeutics (SUD)

Built reSET and reSET-O — FDA-cleared digital therapeutics for substance use disorder. Their early model: paid clinical pilots with health systems that generated the outcomes data for FDA submission. Each pilot was structured as a co-development agreement. The health system funded the pilot; Pear used the data for clearance + fundraising.

Pattern Match

Omada Health (Diabetes)

Launched with paid pilots at major health systems where the health system paid for development and deployment. The outcomes data from those pilots became the basis for their commercial model and Series A raise. They now serve 1,800+ enterprise customers.

Why BayMark Specifically Should Say Yes

BayMark is uniquely positioned to benefit from this pilot in ways their competitors cannot match:

Scale: With 300+ clinics, a successful pilot at 3–5 California sites immediately implies a 60× national rollout opportunity. No other OUD provider has this footprint. BayMark's PE investors will see a $500K pilot as a $30M+ annual revenue opportunity if it scales.

Data advantage: BayMark is already collaborating with California DHCS on outcomes data. A VictoryAI pilot gives them AI-powered predictive analytics and structured engagement data that makes their DHCS reporting 10× more compelling.

Competitive moat: If BayMark doesn't do this, Groups Recover Together or Bicycle Health will. And then BayMark's 300-clinic empire is competing with tech-enabled upstarts who can show better outcomes with smaller teams. This isn't optional — it's defensive.
